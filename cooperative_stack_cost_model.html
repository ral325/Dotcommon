model assumptions
Worker profile
Credit volume is modelled as a fixed percentage of annual income (utilization slider). This implies credit needs scale proportionally with earnings, which is a simplification — in practice, cash flow gaps are driven by job size and timing, not income level directly.

Jobs per year is assumed to be evenly distributed across the year with no seasonality.

Credit draws are assumed to be equal in size and evenly spaced. In practice draw sizes and timing will vary significantly.

All members are assumed to have the same income, job frequency, and credit behaviour. The model represents a single archetypal member, not a distribution.
Transaction & gas costs
Payment tx fees for crypto rails use flat per-transaction estimates based on typical L2 gas prices as of 2024–25. Actual gas costs fluctuate with network congestion and may be higher during peak periods.

ZK proof generation costs (Reclaim, zkPass, TLSNotary) are estimated ranges based on comparable ZK compute benchmarks. None of these protocols have fixed public pricing; treat pessimistic values as planning floors.

ZK on-chain verification gas is estimated at $0.08–$0.60 per proof submission, reflecting the higher computational cost of verifying ZK proofs versus simple token transfers.

Credit pool tx gas assumes two on-chain transactions per draw (draw + repayment) at $0.05–$0.40 each.

Treasury disbursement gas assumes one treasury transaction per four governance votes at $0.10–$0.50 each.

ZK attestation proofs are assumed to be generated twice per year per member (annual re-attestation plus one additional).
Credit rate model
Base rate is a fixed annual cost covering pool liquidity overhead and operating costs. It does not vary with membership size.

Risk premium starts at the value set for 100 members and decays linearly toward zero as membership grows, at a rate set by the scale-down slider. This models the improving information advantage the network gains as its income dataset grows. The floor is zero — the risk premium cannot go negative.

Pool health adjustment is a flat governance-set buffer rate. In a real system this would be raised automatically when default rates exceed a threshold; here it is a manual input.

Effective rate = base rate + (risk premium × scale factor) + health adjustment, applied to credit volume annually. No compounding is modelled — this is a simple annual interest approximation.

Default risk is not explicitly modelled as a cost line. It is implicitly priced into the risk premium. The model does not simulate actual default events or pool drawdowns.
Network-level costs
Smart contract audits are amortised over 5 years regardless of network growth rate. A faster-growing network would amortise this sooner; a stagnant one more slowly.

Aave fork audits are assumed to require only a partial audit of modifications rather than a full codebase review. In practice, auditors often review the full system in context, which may push costs toward the custom pool range.

RPC / node infrastructure is modelled as a base hosting cost plus a per-transaction throughput term: optimistic $600 + (members × jobs × $0.00008), pessimistic $1,200 + (members × jobs × $0.00025).

Legal / compliance costs are modelled as a linear function of membership: optimistic $2,000 + ($1.50 × members), pessimistic $5,000 + ($4.00 × members). In reality legal costs are highly jurisdiction-dependent and may step up nonlinearly at regulatory thresholds.

All network costs are divided equally across members. This assumes uniform cost allocation, which may not reflect a real cooperative's fee structure.
What is not modelled
Pool liquidity reserves — the network must hold capital against simultaneous draws. The cost of holding these reserves (opportunity cost or explicit reserve funding) is not included.

Onboarding costs — the cost of bringing new members onto the platform (KYC-equivalent, initial attestation, onboarding support) is not modelled.

Token volatility — if the network uses a native token for governance or fee payment, token price volatility could materially affect real costs. All costs here are modelled in USD equivalents.

Regulatory compliance costs specific to financial services — operating a mutual credit network may trigger money transmission licensing requirements in some jurisdictions. These costs are not captured beyond the general legal/compliance line.

Development and maintenance costs — the cost of building and maintaining the platform software is not included. This is a significant real cost, particularly in early stages.

Member support and dispute resolution — the operational cost of running Kleros-based or peer-based dispute resolution processes is not modelled.
Traditional stack comparison
Platform fee is fixed at 20% of income, representing a typical marketplace rate (Upwork, Angi, Thumbtack). Actual rates vary by platform and worker tier.

Payment processing is fixed at 2.9% of income, representing Stripe's standard rate. ACH transfers are cheaper; card processing may be higher.

Credit interest is fixed at 22% on the credit volume, representing a typical small business credit card or short-term loan rate. Invoice financing rates vary widely.

Banking fees are fixed at $240/year, representing a typical small business checking account fee. This does not include wire fees, overdraft fees, or other incidental costs.

The traditional stack total is a flat line — it does not benefit from network scale effects, which is a key structural advantage of the cooperative model being shown.
